Q1 Forensic Analysis of a Hit-and-Run Accident ★★★
Guiding Questions
  • 01The percentages given only cover Pb and Cr. What must the remaining percentage represent?
  • 02If you assume exactly 100 g of the compound, what does each percentage directly tell you about the mass of each element?
  • 03How do you convert a mass of an element into moles? What values do you need from the periodic table? (Pb = 207, Cr = 52, O = 16)
  • 04Once you have moles of Pb, Cr, and O, what operation gives you the simplest whole-number ratio?
Step Scaffold
  • 01Find %O by subtraction: 100% − (64.0% + 16.2%).
  • 02Assume 100 g sample — the mass of each element equals its percentage numerically.
  • 03Divide each mass by the element's molar mass to find moles (Pb, Cr, O).
  • 04Divide all mole values by the smallest mole value.
  • 05Round to the nearest integer → write the empirical formula.
Guiding Questions
  • 01PbCrO₄ is described as insoluble in water. What happens when two insoluble substances are mixed — can ions freely exchange in solution?
  • 02What would form on the outer surface of the PbCrO₄ solid if it were placed directly into AgNO₃ solution?
  • 03If a solid coating forms around the PbCrO₄ particles, what effect does this layer have on the reaction reaching completion?
Key Concept
  • ↯A reaction can only proceed if ions are in solution and free to collide. An insoluble product layer on the surface of a solid reactant physically blocks further reaction — this is the passivation or coating effect.
Guiding Questions
  • 01What is the molar mass of PbCrO₄? Add up the atomic masses carefully — remember there are 4 oxygens (Pb = 207, Cr = 52, O = 16).
  • 02How many moles of PbCrO₄ are present in the 2.50 g sample?
  • 03Trace the chromium atom through both steps: PbCrO₄ → Na₂CrO₄ → Ag₂CrO₄. How many CrO₄²⁻ units does each step produce per formula unit?
  • 04What is the mole ratio of PbCrO₄ to Ag₂CrO₄? What is the molar mass of Ag₂CrO₄? (Ag = 108)
Step Scaffold
  • 01M(PbCrO₄) = 207 + 52 + 4(16) = 323 g mol⁻¹
  • 02Moles PbCrO₄ = 2.50 ÷ 323
  • 031 : 1 mole ratio — each PbCrO₄ contains one CrO₄²⁻, and each CrO₄²⁻ yields one Ag₂CrO₄.
  • 04M(Ag₂CrO₄) = 2(108) + 52 + 4(16) = 332 g mol⁻¹
  • 05Theoretical mass = moles × 332
Guiding Questions
  • 01What is the formula for percentage yield? Which value goes in the numerator?
  • 02The actual yield (2.82 g) is larger than your theoretical value. What does a percentage yield above 100% tell you about the experiment?
Formula Reminder
  • ↯% yield = (actual mass ÷ theoretical mass) × 100%. A result >100% is only experimentally possible if the product contains impurities or excess mass was recorded.
Guiding Questions — Below 100%
  • 01The question assumes the reaction runs to completion — but does it? What if some PbCrO₄ was not fully converted by the Na₂CO₃ step?
  • 02During filtration and transfer, is it physically possible to collect every last particle of precipitate without any loss?
Guiding Questions — Above 100%
  • 03If the solid sample contained another chromium compound (not just PbCrO₄), would that extra chromium also react to produce Ag₂CrO₄?
  • 04If the Ag₂CrO₄ precipitate was collected while still damp, what extra mass would appear on the balance?
  • 05Could other ions in solution co-precipitate alongside Ag₂CrO₄ and add to the recorded mass?
Q2 % by Mass of Pb(NO₃)₂ in an Impure Mixture ★★★★
Guiding Questions — Experimental Design
  • 01Both salts dissolve in water, so dissolving won't separate them. What other property of Pb²⁺ ions can you exploit?
  • 02What common lab reagent produces a white insoluble precipitate with Pb²⁺ but not with Na⁺?
  • 03After precipitation: how do you separate the solid from the liquid, and how do you remove trapped soluble salts from the solid?
  • 04What must you do to the solid before weighing to avoid recording the mass of water as part of your precipitate?
Guiding Questions — Calculation
  • 05You measure mass Y g of PbSO₄ precipitate. How do you convert that mass into moles? (M(PbSO₄) = 303.3 g mol⁻¹)
  • 06What is the mole ratio of Pb(NO₃)₂ to PbSO₄ from the balanced equation? Use this to find moles of Pb(NO₃)₂.
  • 07Once you have moles of Pb(NO₃)₂, how do you find its mass, then express it as a percentage of the original mixture?
Step Scaffold
  • 01Weigh the total mixture: mass = X g.
  • 02Dissolve in water; add excess K₂SO₄ solution to precipitate PbSO₄.
  • 03Filter, wash with distilled water, dry in oven, then weigh: mass precipitate = Y g.
  • 04Moles PbSO₄ = Y ÷ 303.3; Moles Pb(NO₃)₂ = same (1 : 1 ratio).
  • 05Mass Pb(NO₃)₂ = moles × 331.2 g mol⁻¹.
  • 06% Pb(NO₃)₂ = (mass Pb(NO₃)₂ ÷ X) × 100%.
Q3 Historical Atomic Mass Standards & the ¹²C Scale ★★★★
Guiding Questions
  • 01On the chemist's scale, natural oxygen (a mixture) is defined as exactly 16. Natural oxygen contains ¹⁶O, ¹⁷O, and ¹⁸O. Which are the heavier isotopes?
  • 02If the weighted average of the mixture is 16, and some members (¹⁷O and ¹⁸O) are heavier than 16, must the lightest member (¹⁶O) be above or below the average?
  • 03Analogy: if the average height of a group is 170 cm and some people are taller than 170 cm, what must be true about the shortest person in the group?
Guiding Questions
  • 01On the physicist's scale, pure ¹⁶O is defined as exactly 16. Natural oxygen also contains ¹⁷O and ¹⁸O, which are heavier than ¹⁶O.
  • 02The relative atomic mass of natural oxygen is a weighted average. If some components of the mixture are heavier than ¹⁶O, is the average greater than, equal to, or less than 16?
Guiding Questions
  • 01If chemists and physicists published the Avogadro constant with slightly different numerical values, what happens when one community uses the other's data in a precise calculation?
  • 02Science is meant to be universal and reproducible. What does having two different standards for the same quantity do to international collaboration and published literature?
Guiding Questions
  • 01When the new standard was adopted, ¹⁶O became 15.9949 instead of 16. Is this a large change? Would scientists need to completely redo their existing data tables?
  • 02Carbon-12 makes up about 99% of all carbon. How does this affect the ease of obtaining a pure sample for calibration?
  • 03Is carbon a solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature? Why does physical state matter when you want a stable, easy-to-handle reference material?
  • 04Carbon forms compounds with almost every other element. Why is this useful for determining relative atomic masses of other elements?
Guiding Questions
  • 3eThe definition says: 1 mole = the amount of substance containing as many entities as atoms in exactly 12 g of ¹²C. So what is the molar mass of ¹²C, directly from the definition?
  • 3fThe Avogadro constant is the number of atoms in 12 g of ¹²C. If you know the mass of one ¹²C atom, how would you calculate that number? Think: total mass ÷ mass per atom.
  • 3gThe Avogadro constant converts moles to number of particles. If you multiply (mol) × (mol⁻¹), what unit do you get? Why is that consistent with a dimensionless "number of particles"?
Guiding Questions
  • 01You are told one ³²S atom is 2.67 times heavier than one ¹²C atom. Does this same ratio apply to a collection of Avogadro's number of each type of atom? Why?
  • 02You know the molar mass of ¹²C from part (e). Multiply it by 2.67. What do you get?
  • 03Does your answer match what you would expect for sulphur-32? This is a useful self-check!
Q4 Titration Curve ★★★★★
Guiding Questions
  • 01The product is NaNO₃. What two reactants combine to give NaNO₃ and water? Identify the acid and the base.
  • 02In a titration, the titrant is in the burette and the analyte is in the conical flask. Which common acid-base indicator is colourless in acid and pink in alkali?
  • 03The colour changes from colourless → pink. Does this mean the solution starts acidic or alkaline? So which substance is in the flask, and which is in the burette?
Guiding Questions
  • 01The equivalence point is about moles — what condition is met when it is reached? Can you directly see this happening?
  • 02The end point is what you actually observe. What causes it to occur? Is it a direct measurement of moles?
  • 03Look at the pH curve. At what part of the curve does the colour change (end point) happen? At what part does pH = 7 (the equivalence point for strong acid + strong base)?
  • 04The curve is near-vertical in the equivalence region. What does this tell you about how close the end point is to the equivalence point in practice?
Initial Flat Region — Low pH
  • 01Low pHpH = −log[H⁺]. At pH 1, what is [H⁺]? At pH 2? How much H⁺ must you remove to raise pH by 1 unit starting at pH 1 vs starting at pH 3?
  • 02Low pHWhen [H⁺] is very high, is it easy or hard to change pH significantly by adding a small amount of NaOH? Why?
Near the Equivalence Point
  • 03Equiv.Just before the equivalence point, almost all H⁺ has been neutralised — [H⁺] is tiny. Adding one more drop of NaOH neutralises what fraction of the remaining H⁺?
  • 04Equiv.On a log scale, does a very small absolute change in [H⁺] (when [H⁺] is already tiny) correspond to a large or small change in pH?
Final Flat Region — High pH
  • 05High pHAfter the equivalence point, excess OH⁻ is present. To raise pH from 11 to 12, how much must [OH⁻] change?
  • 06High pHBy analogy with the initial region — when [OH⁻] is already high, does adding more NaOH change pH dramatically or barely at all?
Core Idea — Link All Three Regions
  • ↯The pH scale is logarithmic. Each unit = a 10-fold change in [H⁺] or [OH⁻]. The same small volume of NaOH causes a massive pH jump near the equivalence point (where [H⁺] is near zero), but barely moves pH when ion concentrations are very high (start or end of the curve).
